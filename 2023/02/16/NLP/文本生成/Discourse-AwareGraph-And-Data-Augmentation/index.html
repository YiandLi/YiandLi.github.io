Posted on 2023-02-16 In NLP , Text Generation
Symbols count in article: 889 Reading time ≈ 4 mins.

Paper: Dialogue Discourse-Aware Graph Model and Data Augmentation for Meeting Summarization

Task：Meeting summarization

Challenges and solutions：

  • lack of sufficient training data -> Data Augmentation
  • a dynamic information exchange flow, dynamic interaction and relationships among utterance -> Discourse-Aware Graph Model

Model: a Dialogue Discourse-Aware Meeting Summarizer (DDAMS)
a relationship graph encoder to encode the utterance relationship

Data Augmentation strategy: a Dialogue Discourse-Aware Data Augmentation (DDADA)
construct a pseudo-summarization corpus

Task definition

input meeting: U\mathcal UU ,
consists of [u1,u2,...u∣U∣][\mathcal u_1,\mathcal u_2,...u_{|\mathcal U| }][u1​,u2​,...u∣U∣​] utterances ,
the i-th utterance could be represented as a sequence of words ui=[ui,1,ui,2,...,ui,∣ui∣]u_i=[u_{i,1}, u_{i,2},...,u_{i,|u_i|} ]ui​=[ui,1​,ui,2​,...,ui,∣ui​∣​]
each utterance correspond to a speaker pi∈Pp_i\in \mathbb Ppi​∈P

output summary Y\mathcal YY , consists of [y1,y2,...y∣Y∣][\mathcal y_1,\mathcal y_2,...y_{|\mathcal Y| }][y1​,y2​,...y∣Y∣​] words

Dialogue Discourse


Dialogue discourse indicates relations between discourse units in a conversation

There are 16 discourse relations [Asher et al., 2016] in total: comment, clarificationquestion, elaboration, acknowledgment, continuation, explanation, conditional, QA, alternation, question-elaboration, result, background, narration, correction, parallel, contrast.

Dialogue Discourse-Aware Meeting Summarizer (DDAMS)

four components: a meeting graph, a vertex representation module, a
graph encoder, and a pointer decoder

Meeting Graph


( Fig2.a ): Use a dialogue parser [Shi and Huang, 2019] to get discourse relationships.

( Fig2.b ): Then apply Levi graph transformation , which turn the labeled edge into additional node , Where (U3, Contrast, U1)\text{(U3, Contrast, U1)}(U3, Contrast, U1) becomes (U3, default, Contrast)\text{(U3, default, Contrast)}(U3, default, Contrast) and (Contrast, default, U1)\text{(Contrast, default, U1)}(Contrast, default, U1) .
Also, there r two edge types are ‘default’ and ‘reverse’ .

( Fig2.c ): To aggregate the non-local information, there is a global vertex connecting all the vertices by global edges.
The global vertex is used to initialize the decoder.
Also, self edges are added to aggregate self-information.

( Fig2.d ): To expand the relationship between discourses, the ‘default’ and ‘reverse’ relation is expanded as ‘default-in-discourse’ , ‘default-out-discourse’ , ‘reverse-in-discourse’ and ‘reverse-out-discourse’ edges.

The final graph is represented as GM=(VM,EM,RM)\mathcal G_M=(\mathbb V_M, \mathbb E_M, \mathbb R_M)GM​=(VM​,EM​,RM​)
RM\mathbb R_MRM​ is the set of edge/relations types, there are totally six types: default-in-discourse, default-out-discourse, reverse-in-discourse, reverse-out-discourse, global, self .

Vertex Representation / utterance encoder

To obtain the initial representations of global node, utterance node and relation node.

For global vertex and relation vertices, use an embedding representation table for initialization.

For utterance node, use Bi-LSTM for embedding.

hi,j→=LSTM⁡f(hi,j−1→,ei,j)hi,j←=LSTM⁡b(hi,j+1←,ei,j)hi,j=[hi,j→;hi,j←]\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{h}_{i, j}}=\operatorname{LSTM}_f\left(\overrightarrow{\boldsymbol{h}_{i, j-1}}, e_{i, j}\right)\\ \overleftarrow{\boldsymbol{h}_{i, j}}=\operatorname{LSTM}_b\left(\overleftarrow{h_{i, j+1}}, e_{i, j}\right)\\ h_{i, j}=\left[\overrightarrow{h_{i, j}} ; \overleftarrow{h_{i, j}}\right] hi,j​​=LSTMf​(hi,j−1​​,ei,j​)hi,j​​=LSTMb​(hi,j+1​​,ei,j​)hi,j​=[hi,j​​;hi,j​​]

hi,jh_{i,j}hi,j​ and ei,je_{i,j}ei,j​ is the hidden representation and the embedding of the word ui,ju_{i,j}ui,j​ .
To involve speaker information, we encode speaker pip_ipi​ using a one-hot vector and get the input ei,je_{i,j}ei,j​ by concatenating word embedding and one-hot speaker embedding.

The utterance representation is hi0=[hi,∣u−i∣→;hi,1←]h_i^0 = \left[\overrightarrow{h_{i, |u-i|}} ; \overleftarrow{h_{i, 1}}\right]hi0​=[hi,∣u−i∣​​;hi,1​​] , as is used as the input to the GNN.

Pointer Decoder

To generate the summary: a standard LSTM decoder + attention + copy mechanism

The global representation is used to initialize the LSTM decoder.

At time step ttt, LSTM give the decoder state sts_tst​, then the word-level attention representation is calculated as:

ei,jt=st⊤Wahi,j0at=softmax⁡(et)htwl=∑i∑jai,jthi,j0\begin{aligned} & e_{i, j}^t=s_t^{\top} \boldsymbol{W}_a \boldsymbol{h}_{i, j}^0 \\ & \boldsymbol{a}^t=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\boldsymbol{e}^t\right) \\ & \boldsymbol{h}_t^{w l}=\sum_i \sum_j a_{i, j}^t \boldsymbol{h}_{i, j}^0 \end{aligned} ​ei,jt​=st⊤​Wa​hi,j0​at=softmax(et)htwl​=i∑​j∑​ai,jt​hi,j0​​

hi,j0\boldsymbol{h}_{i, j}^0hi,j0​ is obtained from Bi-LSTM utterance encoder for the token ui,ju_{i,j}ui,j​ .

The utterance-level representation htul\boldsymbol{h}_t^{u l}htul​ is calculated similarly, except that the final GNN-layer utterance representation is used.

Then for each token, the final context vector is ht∗\boldsymbol{h}_t^{*}ht∗​ , then is used in the Pointer network for the output token either from the fixed-length vocabulary or copy from the input. refer to Get to the point: Summarization with pointer-generator networks.
